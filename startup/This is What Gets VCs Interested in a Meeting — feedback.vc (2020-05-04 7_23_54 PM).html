We Analyzed Over 500 Pitches: This is What Gets a VC Meeting

We built Feedback.vc as a way for founders to turn the frustrating black hole of fundraising into an informative exercise that helps them improve their pitches and the vision for their company. Participants get both quantitative and qualitative feedback across multiple aspects of their business.

We’ve processed over 500 double-blind pitches so far and wanted to see what was driving success. Participants don’t post anything personally identifiable about themselves or their business. They share what they do, not who they are—other than high level bios.

On the other side, an anonymous panel of VCs reviews the information they get, deciding whether or not they want to opt into an introduction. The purpose of the platform is really organizing and standardizing the feedback. The intros are a nice, but not guaranteed, side benefit for some companies.

We analyzed the results of our first 500 reviews and calculated the correlations between high marks in categories like team, problem area and business economics with the number of intros from our reviewing VCs each team got. Overall, high scores on the review were a strong predictor of VC interest, but the results of the scores of the individual categories might surprise you.

Here’s what mattered—and what didn’t, in order of least to most predictive:

Product & Solution - Weak Predictor

It has been said that “ideas are a dime a dozen” and that seems to be the case with VCs. VCs who gave high marks on the various solutions presented only showed slight interest in asking for an intro to the founder.

The Raise - Weak Predictor

Am I raising too little or too much? Am I going to signal something unintentionally based on what I’m asking for? It didn’t really really matter much to our investors. On many occasions, they looked past issues they had with the raise, seemingly because if they got interested in the company, they could influence what they saw as the right amount for the raise.

Team - Moderate Predictor

Team. Team. Team? Yeah, perhaps a good team isn’t everything. Some good teams got lots of intros and others, not so much. On the flip side, there were no good teams that got zero intros. Bad teams definitely sunk your chances—but having a great team didn’t necessarily mean lots of VCs wanted to meet you if they didn’t like the other aspects of your company.

The Basics & Gut Check - Moderate Predictor

We ask teams to describe what they’re working on briefly and immediately ask VCs what they think before they dive in. This was a pretty decent predictor—which means that the next time you’re networking with VCs, if they don’t show interest after that first one liner, you might be better off politely excusing yourself than trying to stay and win them over.

Hypothesis - Moderate Predictor

Agreeing on how you see the world is important—but deciding what’s important about how you see the world is just as critical. We noticed that while some who scored a lot of investor intros had high agreement between both sides as to first principals, others who got investors to agree with their views didn’t get more than a couple of investors kicking the tires. That rings true for any investor who has ever had to say, “That may be true, but that doesn’t mean you have a business here.”

The Problem & Market Segment - Moderate to Strong Predictor

This would have been a stronger predictor had it not been for a few outliers at the top. Some of the companies who got the highest number of intros didn’t necessarily get the highest scores for picking the right problem—they were solid, but not so spectacular to predict their performance with intros. Aside from them, however, this was a very good predictor of who get intros—so picking the right problem to work on seemed more important than having the right solution by more than 2x.

So what was the most important factor?

Which area, if a VC only knew one thing about your company, was the most predictive of enticing a VC to want to meet?

Some founders might be disappointed, because its that age old question that they love to hate.

“How does it make money??”

Yes, the underlying economics of the business was the strongest predictor of whether a VC got interested, over any other aspect of the company. Founders who could clearly articulate an economic opportunity fared really well with investors in terms of getting intros.

Bonus: Gender Math

Our submissions remove any personally identifiable information about the founders—so our investors don’t know which teams have male or female founders. However, it was interesting that women, on average, scored 13% more intros on our platform. That may be because their scores were higher overall—especially in the team area, where they were voted 30% better than their male counterparts when the genders were removed.
