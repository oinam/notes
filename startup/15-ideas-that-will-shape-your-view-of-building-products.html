15 Ideas That Will Shape Your View Of Building Products

Ideas and mental models for product managers

Alex Pedicini
May 26 · 5 min read

1. Start with Why

“People don’t buy what you do they buy why you do it.” Simon Sinek’s leadership lessons provide a great model for leaders and product managers to communicate and inspire. While most leaders start with the What — the product or solution — Sinek advocates for starting with Why — your purpose for existing and the How — your principles and values that guide your actions.

2. Pareto principle

Also known as the 80/20 rule, this is the idea the majority of results come from a minority of causes. Identify the narrow set of features or customers that drive the majority of impact on your business and focus on improving that functionality and delighting those users.

3. Eating your own dogfood

The practice of testing and using your own products so that you can empathize with your customers and identify opportunity areas. This works even better if you are able to test your product in the same environments and circumstances that a typical user would.

4. Velocity

Speed and velocity are not the same things; speed measures how fast you go, regardless of direction, while velocity measures distance displaced. In product development you want velocity, and the direction you want to be traveling is towards your strategy. High velocity requires prioritization and saying no to tasks or projects that are not contributing to the strategy you are executing towards.

5. Second-order thinking

As opposed to first-order thinking, which is primarily about the cause-and-effect of the immediate decision you are facing, second-order thinking involves asking “what happens next?”. Primarily this way of thinking requires you to understand the unintended consequences and effects of any change or decision. The 10–10–10 rule can be particularly helpful to understand the second or third order impact of your decisions.

6. Reversible vs. Irreversible decisions

This has been written about by Jeff Bezos in his 2016 Shareholder Letter. The idea is that there are two types of decisions — reversible, which make up the majority of decisions and can be easily changed later, and irreversible, which are hard to change. Reversible decisions should be made quickly and without perfect information since you can adjust the course quickly. Irreversible decisions should be made carefully and require more extensive information gathering before reaching a conclusion. The key is knowing which type of decision you are making beforehand.

7. Opportunity costs

Every decision comes with a tradeoff — by choosing one thing we are saying no to another. This is especially true when prioritizing or building a roadmap. Sometimes the easiest way to prioritize is to ask “is there anything more valuable for us to do right now?” — if the answer is yes, then get started on that more valuable thing.

8. Paradox of choice

More choices do not always equate to a happier customer. People can be overwhelmed by the options presented to them resulting in indecision and less satisfaction. Instead, focus on smarter defaults and balance presenting the right options to customers at the right times to avoid decision fatigue.

9. Inversion

To get a different perspective on a problem it often helps to think of it in reverse. Instead of thinking through why something will work, think about the reasons it won’t. Instead of forecasting from the current state and projecting forward, try backcasting by thinking backward from your end goal. A helpful technique for fostering this is by conducting a “Premortem” before starting a project.

10. Probabilistic thinking

Product development requires making decisions based on assumptions and imperfect information. Probabilistic thinking is the process of using logic and estimation to guess the outcome of a particular event. This type of thinking helps inform where to place your bets on what problems to solve and which solutions to build.

11. Do things that don’t scale

Software allows us to do things at an incredible scale, but remember that all products and companies started small. Doing things that don’t scale is a reminder that putting in the work manually is often the faster path to learning. It will bring you closer to the problems and customers you are building for and may even illuminate other opportunities that you would otherwise miss. With time and success you can seek to automate these tasks that prove valuable.

12. Secure the beachhead

Geoffrey Moore talked about bridging the technology adoption lifecycle in his book Crossing the Chasm. The book discusses how to move your product from early adopters to mainstream consumers by first identifying and selling to a small segment of your target customer base. This idea is similar to finding niche markets to start with and satisfying that narrow market before broadening your product and customer base.

13. Vitamins vs. Painkillers

Understand if your product a “nice to have” (vitamin) or a “must-have” (painkiller). The key to product positioning and selling is identifying the real pain point your product solves, who experiences the problem, and the “trigger” that causes the problem.

14. Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs

This theory describes the five basic human motivations and needs. Human needs rarely change, though the solutions used to achieve those needs and desires can change often. You can tie every successful software product back to satisficing at least of one of these basic needs.

15. Theory of constraints

“A chain is no stronger than its weakest link.” Identifying the bottleneck and working to reduce or remove that constraint can improve throughput. This model can apply to any system at work — whether that's in the product you are building or in a process you are relying upon.
